This week I had one tab full of agents, workflow cleanup, and CRM logic.

The next tab had Bir, Aasha, player-picked names, and me fussing over how five complete levels were supposed to feel instead of just function.

That is not a mature workflow. I recommend it anyway.

The respectable side of my workspace is easy to explain. Agents triage work. Automations keep follow-ups from slipping. Research pipelines turn vague intention into sequence. Useful software everywhere, standing up straight, answering questions properly, wearing its little business-casual badge.

Then there is Doko Dash.

Doko Dash is a web-first 2D platformer I am building around terrace fields, rice bowls, momo pickups, touch controls, Bir, Aasha, player-picked names, and five complete levels.

It is not just one tiny graybox proof anymore. The shape is bigger now. Multiple characters. Players get to name them. Five complete levels. More real decisions. More places for something to feel delightful or annoying.

There is no respectable enterprise explanation for why I care whether Bir and Aasha feel distinct, whether player naming adds charm instead of friction, or whether the fifth level earns its difficulty instead of becoming a jerk.

I care anyway.

That has been weirdly good for me.

If I stay in useful-work mode too long, every new idea starts showing up like it has a fake MBA.

What problem do you solve. What workflow do you improve. Can you justify your budget before you sit down.

I start interviewing ideas instead of liking them.

That mindset is not useless. I have built a lot of good things from it. It makes me sharper. It makes me cut cleaner. It makes me pretty good at spotting nonsense.

It also makes me a little exhausting.

Doko Dash cannot survive that interview.

A graybox platformer with rice bowls and momo pickups is not going to win a conference room debate about strategic value. It is not here to improve pipeline hygiene. It is not here to automate my life. It is not secretly a productivity tactic wearing a fake mustache.

It is here because I wanted something bright in a workspace that had gotten extremely good at being competent.

One of the funniest parts of this project is how quickly my brain tries to drag it back into adulthood.

I open the game and think I am about to do something unserious. Then five minutes later I am arguing with myself about whether Bir and Aasha feel meaningfully different, whether player-picked names make the whole thing feel more personal, whether the touch buttons are thumb-friendly without hiding too much of the level, whether the first monkey shows up too early, and whether the later levels ramp up like a challenge instead of a punishment.

That is still design work.

It just has a pulse.

Useful systems work usually asks me to be skeptical. Where does this fail. What should get cut. What breaks first. What sequence holds.

Doko Dash asks a different question.

Does this feel good yet.

Not profitable. Not defensible. Not scalable. Good.

That sounds softer than it is. It is actually a pretty annoying question because it refuses to hide behind logic. A bad jump feels bad. Awkward touch controls feel awkward. A level that teaches the wrong thing in the wrong order does not care how disciplined your project tracker is.

You either made the thing pleasant to play or you did not.

I like that kind of honesty.

It gets me out of explanation mode.

I had not realized how much of my recent work lived in explanation mode. Agents. automations. CRM flows. research pipelines. All valid. All useful. All requiring a clean reason to exist.

After enough of that, your brain starts showing up in a tie every morning.

Everything becomes a case. A sequence. A failure mode. A budget line. You can feel yourself getting more efficient and a little less alive at the same time.

That is the trap for me.

Not useful work itself. I like useful work. Some of it pays me. Some of it saves me from chaos. Some of it has taught me more about software than any class ever could.

The trap is letting useful work take every slot on the board until every new idea has to walk in, clear its throat, and present a business case before it is allowed to exist.

I also do not want to oversell the romance of fun projects because that gets fake fast.

I know the bad version of this story too. Endless concept art. Grand lore for a game that does not survive ten minutes of actual play. New engine detours because starting over feels more glamorous than fixing jump feel. A heroic future roadmap for a thing that does not yet deserve one.

I already had my little side quest with Phaser. It got evaluated, rejected, and politely escorted out of the building. Godot is the lane now. That part of the project is not mysterious anymore.

The current rule set is delightfully unglamorous.

Stop chasing engines. Stop adding nonsense just because it sounds impressive. Get movement feel right. Get touch controls right. Make the actual levels worth playing. Then earn the next decision.

That discipline is part of why Doko Dash feels so clean to me.

It is playful, but it is not sloppy.

It is still restrained on purpose. Not because it stayed tiny, but because it has not turned into a fake empire. Bir and Aasha are enough. Five complete levels are enough. Letting players pick names is enough. I do not need a hundred systems, twelve worlds, and a lore bible thick enough to injure someone before the thing is genuinely fun on desktop and phone.

Honestly, that restraint might be the sassiest part of the whole project.

It does not need to be important. It just needs to be real.

That difference matters.

When a project does not owe me a business case, I can pay attention to weirder and more useful things. Rhythm. Texture. Readability. Whether a level teaches gently before it starts being rude. Whether a checkpoint arrives like relief instead of paperwork.

That is not wasted effort. That is me remembering I like craft.

I think that is what I had gotten a little too far from. Not fun in the childish sense. Fun in the alive sense. Fun in the sense that I open the file because I want to see what happens if I fix one more thing.

That feeling had gotten rarer than I wanted to admit.

So no, I am not going to spin Doko Dash into a neat sermon about how all playful projects secretly increase productivity. Maybe it helps my other work. Maybe it loosens something useful in my head. Fine.

But I do not want to smuggle fun back into my life disguised as an optimization tactic. That is too sad. That is how you end up forcing a momo to submit quarterly goals.

Sometimes the honest reason to build something is just that it pulls on you.

Right now I want one corner of my workspace where Bir and Aasha can move through terrace fields, players can pick names, rice bowls and momos can matter more than dashboards, and five complete levels can exist without once stopping to explain their market size.

That project is not less serious to me because it is playful.

If anything, it is serious in a way I had started neglecting.

It is keeping some oxygen in the room.

Not everything I build has to be useful.

Some things can just be alive enough to make me want to come back tomorrow.
